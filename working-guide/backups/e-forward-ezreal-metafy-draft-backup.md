# Tempo Ezreal

## Guide Overview

I think Tempo Ezreal belongs in the S tier alongside the Draven variants and Irelia. I wrote this guide before the bannings were announced, and those changes ended up being excellent news for this deck. In my view, the post-ban metagame is perfectly set up for Tempo Ezreal to take over.

I’m a former Magic: The Gathering Pro, and tempo decks are the archetype I know best. I spent years near the top of the **MTG Elo Project** Legacy rankings on the back of **RUG Delver**, the defining tempo deck of that format, and this deck scratches that same itch. It is a high-velocity deck full of cheap, flexible cards that let you constantly pivot between aggression and control based on the openings your opponent gives you.

That flexibility is exactly why I believe this deck is so strong. It is probably the hardest deck in the game to play well, but it is also one of the hardest decks in the game to play against. Your spells are absurdly cheap, your cards combine in a huge number of ways, and your opponent has to respect both ends of your deck at all times. They have to keep up on points so you do not run away with the game, while also respecting the game-ending top end of **Thousand-Tailed Watcher** and **Singularity**.

#### What This Guide Covers

This guide is meant to offer an entry point for learning Tempo Ezreal. I cover the current list, the core gameplan, quick mulligan and gameplay heuristics, the key card patterns that actually matter in real games, and detailed matchup notes across the format so you can understand both how the deck works and how it wins.

For the brewers who want to make the deck their own and keep adjusting it post-ban, I have also included notes at the end on top-tier alternatives to many of the cards in my current list. Those options were competitive even in the pre-ban metagame, and they become even more relevant if you want to tune the deck for what comes next.

This also means the guide is worth sharing. The deck is underplayed, difficult, and much stronger than it looks on first pass. Most people who lose to it still will not understand why they lost. That is exactly the kind of deck worth learning early and worth passing along to anyone who likes high-skill, high-ceiling strategies.

### Core Gameplan

Riftbound is about points, and the size of the unit scoring them usually does not matter. This deck is built to use small, efficient cards to fight for points early, build incremental advantages, and stay flexible enough to pivot between aggressive and controlling roles depending on the texture of your hand.

You do have the tools to play a long, fair game when needed. The strength of the deck is that your opponent has to respect both plans at once. You are not always trying to get ahead immediately and snowball from turn one. More often, you are trying to identify the critical turn where you can put your opponent on the back foot, then squeeze them from there and close the game out gradually.

### Ezreal: Legend and Champion

It is important to separate what the legend and champion are each doing in this deck.

#### Ezreal, Prodigal Explorer

The legend is what lets the deck fly through itself and recoup the cost of casting multiple cheap spells every turn. In practice, Ezreal legend is mostly about card velocity. Once you get to around six or seven runes in play, it often feels like you are drawing an extra card almost every turn.

That matters because this deck is full of small, synergistic cards. You are often spending two cards to create one highly efficient exchange, and the legend is what makes that sustainable over the course of a game.

#### Ezreal, Prodigy

The champion is the one that gives you looting. Unlike more controlling Ezreal decks, you usually do not want to deploy Ezreal early just because you can. You would rather wait until there is a card in your hand you know you no longer need, then trade that card for two fresh looks.

Together, the champion and legend keep you fueled so you do not run out of gas, even while you are constantly deploying units and interacting on board.

The cost reduction is real, but in this deck it is more of an afterthought than the reason you are playing Ezreal. It is strong, and because Ezreal often dies on sight you want to take advantage of it when you can, but the bigger role he plays is keeping your hand moving. If you have already played a strong early game without him, that often creates the perfect window to deploy Ezreal and immediately convert the passive into tempo.

### Small Units

Riftbound cares about points, not about whether the unit scoring them is large or small. That makes cheap units with flexible text extremely valuable, and this deck is built to exploit that better than almost anything else in the format.

#### Plundering Poro

Plundering Poro is the best first unit in the deck in most games. The Gold it generates creates an advantage later, and it allows you to cast your most impactful cards without spending Runes in the early turns.

#### Sneaky Deckhand

Sneaky Deckhand is the only unit in the game that can score a point the turn you play it at no Power cost. That effect is extremely powerful, and it is core to how this deck turns the corner.

On turns one through four, a well-timed Deckhand can swing the points race in your favor without asking you to give up your Power development. Many spawn-camp matchups are defined by repeatedly Gusting or Windsingering your own Sneaky Deckhand to score points in ways that are very difficult to interact with cleanly.

#### Windsinger

Windsinger is one of the most flexible cards in the deck. It can generate an Energy advantage by functioning like Gust stapled to a body that can also score points, it can remove your own units from battlefields to rebuy Fizz or Ezreal effects, and it can act as a hidden Gust that protects your units from removal.

That flexibility is a huge part of what makes the deck work.

#### Ravenbloom Student

Ravenbloom Student is the weakest small unit in the deck, but it still fills an important role. If you can play it to a battlefield and hold for even one turn in the early game, the fact that this deck casts two spells so often means Student can play like a conditional Noxus Hopeful and apply real pressure very quickly.

### Efficient Removal

Almost all of your removal is tuned to answer small units efficiently. With Bellows Breath, Wages of Pain, Gust, and Windsinger, you have a huge amount of interaction for anything low to the ground, which makes this deck excellent at fighting over the early game.

Because this deck is playing the points race, bounce is often just as good as killing a unit. If the opponent cannot replay that unit in a way that matters before the game ends, then removing it from the battlefield accomplished the same thing. Bellows Breath is the perfect card to punish opponents on that axis, because if they try to replay too many units in one turn after you bounce them, Bellows can blow them out.

### Shrink Effects

Your shrink effects are what let your efficient removal scale up. They are the enablers that make Gust, Wages of Pain, Windsinger, and Bellows Breath continue to matter once the opposing units stop being small.

That is especially important because this deck is often trying to close the game out, not answer every threat forever. Against a card like Ferrous Forerunner, you do not always need to kill it permanently. If you can shrink it, bounce it, and keep the pressure on, the game often ends before it can come back down profitably.

Ezreal legend is excellent here, because spending two spells to answer one opposing 4-6 Might unit would normally cost too much cardboard. In this deck, it often does not.

### Consistency Cards

The deck is built around having the right combinations of cards at the right time, so it needs glue. Stacked Deck and Fizz are a huge part of that. They help you find the missing half of your interaction, your pressure, or your finishing sequence, and they are a big reason the deck feels cohesive instead of clunky.

### Haymakers

The core of this deck is cheap, synergistic, small-ball Magic. Most of your hand wants to be live, efficient, and easy to pair with something else, so you do not want many expensive cards clogging your draws.

That makes your haymaker slots extremely important. This is the section of the list I am most willing to change between events, and I devote a lot of sideboard space to making sure I have access to the right haymakers in the matchups where they matter most.

#### Thousand-Tailed Watcher

Watcher is one of the best aggressive top ends in the game and is incredible at taking you from six to eight. It ends games immediately, combines with Bellows Breath to sweep up massive boards, and gives the deck a clean way to cash in a lead before the opponent can stabilize.

I would never leave home without two Watchers in my deckbox.

#### Singularity

Your deck is already excellent against swarms of small units. The way opponents usually beat you is by putting multiple 4- and 5-Might units into play at once, because that strains the normal combinations your deck uses to interact.

Singularity is the perfect counter to that plan, and Ezreal legend even helps refund the exchange.

#### Vex, Cheerless

Honestly, Vex does not naturally play well with the rest of the strategy. One of the strengths of this deck is that it usually does not present good targets for cards like Rebuke, Charm, or Challenge, and Vex changes that while also putting you behind a Rune if it dies immediately.

That said, it does a couple of very important jobs. It helps you set up huge pop-off attacks that crack Miracle Draven holds, and it helps cover one of the biggest holes in the deck, which is Deflect. When your plan involves targeting opposing units multiple times to get them off the battlefield, the Vex discount is what keeps that plan functioning.

#### Rebuke

Most of your interaction relies on opposing units having a reasonable amount of Might. Rebuke does not care about that. It also cannot be countered by Defy, which makes it especially important against green decks.

Rebuke is one of your best ways to retrick an oversized equipped unit or answer a battlefield monstrosity that your normal interaction cannot line up against cleanly.

### Battlefield Plan

This deck uses almost every battlefield in the game well, so I have chosen battlefields that are generic, powerful for me, and give the opponent as little as possible.

#### Void Gate (Game 1)

Ezreal Control usually presents Void Gate in game 1, so I do the same thing here to help obfuscate what kind of Ezreal deck I actually am.

Because almost my entire deck is made up of 2-Might units, Void Gate rarely benefits the opponent in a meaningful way. On the other hand, it meaningfully improves cards like Bellows Breath and Wages of Pain, which makes it a very clean fit for this shell.

#### The Arena's Greatest (Play)

On the play, The Arena's Greatest lets this deck do things that feel genuinely unfair with Sneaky Deckhand. The battlefield helps enable the kinds of curves where you can start forcing through points immediately and convert that pressure into a game that ends quickly.

That matters because on the play, you often want your Gust effects to function offensively as virtual card advantage. If the game stays short and your opponent is constantly reacting, those bounce effects become devastating.

#### Reaver's Row (Draw)

Reaver's Row is ridiculous and honestly deserved to be banned. I use it on the draw because that is where this deck is least able to spend extra Energy protecting units while still developing, and Reaver's Row lets the same body keep conquering over and over again.

Playing Sneaky Deckhand to Reaver's Row is one of the best ways in the game to keep up on the draw, and it often feels completely unreasonable. I am glad this battlefield is gone, and this deck has good replacement options, which I will get into later in the card options section.

## How To Pilot The Deck

### Common Heuristics

If you want the quick cheat sheet version of this deck, start here. These are the baseline mulligan and gameplay rules I use most often. The later sections go deeper, but this is enough to get you started without reading every detail first.

#### Mulligan Heuristics

- Your ideal opener has one unit you can play on turn 1, one interactive spell, and one shrink effect. If you do not have that mix, send back two cards.
- Send back cards with a Power cost so your hand stays full of cheap early-game cards and lets you find your more powerful cards later. The main exception is matchup-specific haymakers, such as keeping Rebuke or Vex against Irelia.
- A second unit is good, especially if it is Windsinger or Sneaky Deckhand.
- Do not keep two units and still mulligan two cards. That usually leaves you with too many units early and not enough spells.

#### Gameplay Heuristics

- Always try to start your turn with at least one unit in play. That keeps you in the points race.
- If you can run to base, do it. This deck has to work hard and spend spells to hold battlefields, so you usually do not want to linger there.
- Do not rely on shrink spells to win combat. We rarely let opponents use removal profitably, so trying to set up spots where your Poro fights a 0-Might Draven often gets you blown out instead.
- Get to 6 before your opponent. You win at 8, but once you reach 6 your opponent usually has to start overextending on defense. In my experience, with this deck the first player to 6 wins at least 80% of games.
- Spend Power sparingly. This deck can spend huge amounts of Energy for multiple turns in a row without running out of cards, so the more Runes you keep in play, the better. If you have two reasonable lines, favor the one that costs less Power.

### Early Turn Priorities

Your first turn should always be spent putting a unit into play. Windsinger is a completely fine turn-1 play here. Even though you are giving up the enters effect, scoring points early is more important.

The biggest mistake players make with this deck is treating it like a normal aggro deck, when the goal is not to score points as quickly as possible but to score more points than the opponent.

Once you establish a unit, the early turns come down to three priorities:

#### Deny Your Opponent Points

Your first job is to stop your opponent from holding. It is impossible to pull ahead on points in a turn cycle if your opponent holds a battlefield.

Windsinger is the most important card in this fight. You can fight over your opponent's conquer, and even if they fight back and win the exchange, you can untap and Windsinger their unit before it holds. That lets you develop your own battlefield while still leaving enough Runes up to fight over their next conquer as well.

#### Use Spells To Hold

Holding is key to generating tempo in this deck, but you do not hold in the traditional way by simply having more Might. The most common hold pattern is taking both battlefields, then playing enough defense that your opponent can only take one of them back. You can then retreat that unit and use it to set up another double conquer on the following turn.

Sneaky Deckhand is hands down the best card for generating these turns. You can spend a full turn's Runes on playing defense, then deploy Deckhand directly to a battlefield.

#### Preserve Runes

In the early turns, preserving your Runes matters because this deck can spend all of its Energy every turn in the late game without running out of cards. In general, avoid recycling Runes unless the payoff is dramatic. The main exceptions are Bellows Breath or Singularity turns that leave your opponent with zero units in play, because those swings usually buy enough tempo to justify the cost.

### Key Card Patterns

#### Gust

Gust is Reaction Speed and only costs 1, which makes it the best card in the deck for winning exchanges. When possible, start with slower interaction first so that if the opponent tries to react, you can punish them by Gusting the unit back to hand.

Gust also matters because it can target your own units. Fizz, Sneaky Deckhand, Windsinger, and Ezreal can all be saved from removal this way, rebuying their entry effects at the same time.

#### Windsinger

Windsinger is often simple in the early game. You bounce the opponent's unit, stop them from holding, and keep developing your own board. That alone would make it a strong card, but the real strength is how many roles it can fill in the late game.

You can target your own units immediately after they conquer to rebuy an entry effect or protect them from combat. You can even rebuy Sneaky Deckhand and move it to the other battlefield, which creates turns that are very difficult for the opponent to keep up with.

The hidden mode is less common, but it is especially powerful against decks on the extreme ends of the spectrum.

Against spell-heavy decks, if you play Sneaky Deckhand to an open battlefield and then hide Windsinger there, it becomes extremely difficult for the opponent to clear that battlefield with spells. If they try, you often end up with Sneaky back in hand while still holding with Windsinger.

Against aggressive unit-based decks like Fiora or Rek'Sai, hiding Windsinger early can let you preserve enough Energy to play defense and keep them from conquering over you.

#### Ezreal, Prodigy

Most Ezreal decks are abusing the cost reduction and care about getting their Ezreal in play as quickly as possible. This deck is the opposite. It really cares about the card filtering Ezreal provides on enter, and for that reason you want to save Ezreal as long as possible.

This deck draws a lot of cards, and it is usually not until later in the game that you know which ones you no longer need and can safely discard.

In most matchups, the card you are happiest to pitch is a redundant two-drop that no longer matters. Ezreal is strongest when he is turning dead cardboard into two fresh looks, not when he is entering play early without a clear discard.

## Matchups and Sideboarding

With the bans, the metagame is going to shift. Because of that, I am not going to give a rigid sideboard map for every matchup. Instead, I am going to focus on the broader strategic questions: what matters in each matchup, which cards tend to overperform, which cards tend to underperform, and what kinds of exchanges you should be trying to create.

I am also covering a wider range of matchups than I would have before the bans. The goal is for this guide to stay useful even as the format changes, so you can adapt your list and sideboarding to whatever decks actually emerge.

### Matchup Guide

### Draven Midrange

**My evaluation:** This is the hardest of the big three. Against a medium to good pilot, my experience is that the matchup is heavily favored. Against an excellent Draven pilot who knows this deck well, I think it swings to a slight underdog. It is also one of the more die-roll dependent matchups, and I have not tested it as much as I would like against top-end Draven players.

#### Cards the Matchup Hinges On

**Spinning Axe**  
This is the card the matchup revolves around most often. I frequently shrink their units down to exactly 3 Might, and Spinning Axe punishes that pattern hard. Because the bonus sticks around, it also forces me to answer the equipped unit on my own turn if I want the Axe to stop mattering.

**Falling Star / Piercing Light**  
These are the cards that push me into the control role. Each of them can wipe my battlefield presence with a single spell, which makes overextending dangerous. The upside is that these spells cost them an entire turn of Runes, so if I stay disciplined I can often convert my extra Energy into a gradual advantage.

**Fight or Flight**  
This is another key card in the matchup, but I actually line up well against it. Bellows Breath and Singularity punish Fight or Flight extremely cleanly, so one of the strengths of my deck here is that one of their best payoff cards often walks directly into my best answers.

#### General Gameplan

Defense is key here. I do not want to play a second unit just because I can if that same Energy could instead stop them from scoring. At the same time, I have to respect Brynhyr punishing me for sitting back with too many Runes in the midgame.

My goal is to keep their base under control with Bellows Breath and Singularity on my own turn, then force them into awkward development patterns. A lot of the matchup comes down to baiting them into a Brynhyr turn when I already have enough shrink effects lined up to stop it from attacking profitably.

I also do not want to give up exchanges for free. If I let them win clean combats too often, they pull ahead on cards and their midgame becomes much harder to contain.

#### Sideboarding

**Good cards**  
Extra shrink effects are excellent here, and Smoke Screen is especially good because it helps fight the raw stat increase from Spinning Axe. Rebuke is also strong, because I want access to two copies for Axe units and Forerunner-sized threats, but I usually do not want the third. Singularity is one of the best cards in the matchup because it creates a huge Energy advantage and can erase two turns of development. Arcane Shift is also the kind of effect I want here, because double-spelling their larger units is one of the cleanest ways to keep them under control.

**Bad cards**  
I trim excess 2-drops here because I usually do not want to expose a second cheap unit unless I know it is safe. On the draw, I value Student more because it helps hold. On the play, I value Poro more because the Gold matters. Sneaky Deckhand stays because of how powerful it is early, but starting around turn 3 it becomes one of the weakest cards in the deck, and I would not be surprised if that card gets trimmed even harder with more testing against strong Draven pilots.

### Draven Miracle

**My evaluation:** Mildly favored. If I choose to hedge hard for it with **Unchecked Power**, I think the matchup becomes heavily favored.

**Note:** I started this guide before the bans, and I am leaving this matchup in for posterity and in case a similar **Battering Ram / Rhasa the Sunderer** strategy shows up again in the future.

For this section and the matchup sections that follow, I may mention cards that are not in my exact current list. That is intentional. My posted list is tuned for the pre-ban metagame, while the `good cards` and `bad cards` notes are meant to be broader guidance about what kinds of effects matter in the matchup, not a rigid sideboard prescription.

#### Cards the Matchup Hinges On

**Draven, Vanquisher**  
This is their entire early-game plan. Always plan an answer for Draven before it can take a battlefield. If Draven gets there, they can start playing units directly to a battlefield and set up an early hold much faster than they should be able to.

**Thousand-Tailed Watcher + Bellows Breath**  
You will eventually need to sweep up a large battlefield, and this deck has the perfect tools for doing that. Watcher gives you the body that ends the game, and Bellows Breath is what lets you turn a big swing into a full collapse on their side.

#### General Gameplan

Their plan is to use Draven as a roadblock that buys them enough time to set up their bigger turns. Your plan is to play all-out aggressive and get to 6 while they are still at 1 or 2 points, forcing them to try to hold much earlier than they want to.

The most important thing is answering Draven before it can take a battlefield. Once they get to hide Fight or Flight behind it, the exchange becomes much harder. If Draven gets Gusted, they often do not have time to put it back into play.

It is also worth spending Power early on cards like Bellows Breath and Singularity if that leaves them with no units in play. If they cannot keep a body on board, they are often forced into awkward Bushwhack turns just to get back to a battlefield.

The matchup usually hinges on one huge swing turn. Vex, Watcher, and Bellows Breath give you the tools to clear both battlefields in one sequence. If you have Watcher ready, you can often clear one battlefield and hold it for a turn because they usually do not accelerate well enough to recover immediately.

#### Sideboarding

**Good cards**  
I like overindexing on late-game cards in this matchup. They often do not put up much of a fight in the early game, so I care more about having powerful effects that crack their late-game setup than I do about maximizing small-ball efficiency. Extra Watcher, Singularity, and any dedicated hoser like Unchecked Power all fit that plan well.

**Bad cards**  
Rebuke is one of the first cards I look to trim here. Wages of Pain is also worse than normal, because it is your most expensive small answer and this matchup is less about grinding tiny exchanges than it is about making sure their payoff turns never stick.

### Irelia

**My evaluation:** With 2 maindeck Vex, I think this matchup is heavily favored. With zero copies of Vex in the deck, I think it becomes a slight underdog.

#### Cards the Matchup Hinges On

**Rebuke vs. Not So Fast**  
This is the card exchange that defines the matchup. My normal gameplan does not line up well against Deflect creatures, but Rebuke singlehandedly ruins protect-the-queen strategies like Irelia if it resolves. They only have one spell that cleanly answers it, and I have 3 Fizz to buy it back. My rune base is on 7 Chaos largely because this matchup asks me to cast Rebuke, then Rebuke again, then Fizz back another Rebuke if needed.

**Vex, Cheerless**  
Vex is what lets my normal gameplan function against Deflect creatures. It makes their Not So Fasts much harder to use efficiently and lets me cast my interaction in spots that would otherwise be completely awkward. It is a true counter card here. My list plays 2 Vex because the expected tournament field was roughly half Irelia. If not for this matchup, I would play zero copies.

**Sneaky Deckhand**  
Early points are everything in this matchup for both players. The difference is that I have Gust and Windsinger to keep their Deckhands under control, while they do not have similarly clean tools against mine.

#### General Gameplan

There are two main kinds of games.

In the first type, they fight hard to protect an early creature from your interaction. These games often come down to whether they can keep winning those first exchanges. If they eventually run out of protection and you win one fight with Gust, they usually fall far behind, because you can score 4 points while they spend time rebuilding. From there, you often hit 6 before they do and the game becomes much easier to control. Even if they protect their early units successfully, they usually burn through enough pump spells that later in the game you can keep them off 6 by killing their units, paying through Deflect, or simply bouncing them multiple times. In these games, an accelerated Watcher often kills Irelia cleanly.

In the second type, they commit to playing Irelia early. When that happens, I should usually be at 3 or 4 points before they score their second. In those games, I am happy to let them take both battlefields for stretches while I keep taking my one point a turn and dig for Rebuke and Vex. If I am slightly behind the race to 6, I can use Rebuke offensively to steal back the initiative, though I have to be cautious of Ride the Wind. Once I get ahead, I often do not even care if they send the unit home with Fight or Flight. I just need the battlefield empty for one turn.

I also conquer with Fizz or Ezreal a lot in this matchup, because both can be Gusted back to hand while still helping me dig for the cards that actually matter. Ezreal is especially useful here because I can often bait out a Charm on sight, either to protect Vex later or to rebuy Ezreal's card draw by Gusting it back to safety. Most Irelia players Charm Ezreal immediately, even though the passive discount matters very little in this matchup.

#### Sideboarding

**Good cards**  
Rebuke and Vex are the cards that matter most. So few cards truly swing this matchup that once I find those, they often struggle to beat them. Hard Bargain is also excellent because it protects Rebuke and plays well in the early counter wars.

**Bad cards**  
Bellows Breath is bad here. Any equipment turns it off, it really only matters in the first kind of game, and it costs Power in a matchup where I often want to test the waters first and see whether they are actually protecting a unit. Wages of Pain is also a little clunky because it costs too much Energy to start a fight, so I usually trim one.

### Ezreal Control

**My evaluation:** This matchup is extremely favored. When played correctly, I do not think their deck can keep up with the number of Sneaky Deckhands I can present over the course of the game.

#### Cards the Matchup Hinges On

**Bellows Breath**  
Unlike Falling Star, Bellows Breath has a location restriction. That matters a lot. I want to play a single unit to base, then on the following turn use Sneaky Deckhand to put units in all three locations. I have to stay conscious of Bellows Breath the entire game, but I have the tools to position around it.

**Sneaky Deckhand**  
This is the card that actually breaks the matchup open. Wages of Pain or Arcane Shift cost them 3 Energy, which means they constantly have to leave up Runes just to threaten an answer. That lets me develop in other ways, bait them into spending Energy in the wrong places, then deploy Sneaky Deckhand and immediately Windsinger it to safety.

#### General Gameplan

The matchup is about playing for an Energy advantage. I always want a unit at base, and if they leave up 3 or more Runes I only want to move to a battlefield if I am setting up a position that is still strong after a Wages of Pain.

The main thing I am trying to build toward in the midgame is a Sneaky Deckhand backed by hidden Windsinger. They need two spells to stop you from scoring, and even then you end up with a Sneaky back in hand.

Beyond that, there really is not much nuance. They cannot protect themselves well from my Wages of Pain, they do not put enough pressure on me to punish spending Energy on removal, and they cannot overdevelop to base because Bellows Breath keeps that in check. I get to define the pace of the game, and they spend most of the matchup trying and failing to stabilize.

#### Sideboarding

**Good cards**  
Retreat is excellent because it protects Deckhand while also creating an Energy advantage. Singularity is also strong because it makes sure they never stabilize a battlefield. In broader builds, Arcane Shift is good here too, though the matchup is favorable enough that I do not think I actually need it.

**Bad cards**  
Vex is bad here. Ravenbloom Student also fails to do enough in the matchup. It does not reliably get big enough through their removal, and it does not bring enough else to justify the slot.

One important note: Dr. Mundo is basically the only clean way to lose this matchup, so make sure you leave yourself access to Rebuke.

### Kai'Sa

**My evaluation:** This is the worst matchup. I think we have to play very well just to stay in the game, and even then I would still call it about a 30-40% underdog. That said, I have found a few gameplans that work consistently enough that I would not be shocked if heavy testing against a great Kai'Sa pilot eventually flipped the matchup. We have a lot of moves, and their deck is extremely linear.

#### Cards the Matchup Hinges On

**Hard removal**  
Keeping their score low is everything. I cannot afford to spend Gust freely on their cheap units because Gust is too important against Forerunner tokens and for protecting Fizz or Sneaky Deckhand later. Bellows Breath and Wages of Pain do most of the heavy lifting here, and those are the cards I most want to rebuy with Fizz.

**Ferrous Forerunner**  
In most matchups I am happy to Gust this card, but this matchup is so slow that I often have to fight through it instead. I do have the tools, but multiple early Forerunners are one of the biggest ways the matchup gets out of control. I only want to spend a bounce spell on Forerunner if I can clearly see a path to ending the game in the next few turns. Otherwise, I need to just work through it.

**Sneaky Deckhand vs. Hextech Ray**  
This is one of the most important patterns in the matchup. They have limited Action Speed removal, so my plan is often to play one unit per turn and force them to either spend Falling Star on a single unit or use Hextech Ray to stop me from scoring. Once they run out of Action Speed removal, I can replay and rebuy Sneaky Deckhand over and over until I reach 8, without ever exposing it to Falling Star. Fizz buying back Gust on my own Deckhand is completely reasonable here.

#### General Gameplan

This matchup is glacial, but the early turns still matter because I have to keep their score as low as possible. I do not mind spending Runes to keep them under control, because the game usually goes long enough that I will eventually get to 12 anyway. What matters is making sure they run out of units before I run out of ways to answer them.

I also need a plan for Kai'Sa herself. A lot of the time that means setting up an early Fizz so I can rebuy a Wages of Pain or Bellows Breath that I already spent managing their early creatures.

While I am keeping their board under control, I want to keep testing the waters by trying to score one point at a time. They usually want to spend Hextech Ray to stop that rather than spend Falling Star on a single unit sitting back at base. That means I should go slowly, present one threat at a time, and make them spend their Action Speed answers inefficiently. Once those are gone, the matchup changes. At that point I start replaying Sneaky Deckhand and I do not leave it in play unless I have Gust ready to protect it.

#### Sideboarding

**Good cards**  
Retreat is excellent because it is effectively more copies of Sneaky Deckhand, which is exactly how I want to end the game once I have stabilized. In broader builds, Arcane Shift with Fizz is very strong because it helps churn through Ferrous Forerunners. If I wanted a real trump for the matchup, I would look hard at Dr. Mundo, because Kai'Sa struggles to get it off the battlefield. With Reaver's Row gone, I also expect Sprite Call to be one of the best cards in the matchup, since it scores a point, kills a unit, and can be rebought with Fizz.

**Bad cards**  
A lot of my own units are the weak links here. Vex is abysmally bad, and Ravenbloom Student does not reliably get big enough through Falling Star while also failing to do enough else to justify the slot.

### Viktor

**My evaluation:** Extremely favored.

#### Cards the Matchup Hinges On

**Sneaky Deckhand**  
This is another Wages of Pain matchup, so the goal is to set up Sneaky Deckhand while they have no units at base and protect it with hidden Windsinger or Gust. Once that pattern starts working, they struggle to stop you from scoring efficiently.

#### General Gameplan

Their deck usually plays Seals and does not draw cards especially well, so a lot of the matchup comes down to simply running them out of cardboard. Kill everything, keep their points low, and make them work for every removal spell they spend on your units.

Sneaky Deckhand is one of the best cards in the matchup again, but the game is not only about Deckhand. I can also use bounce effects on Fizz or Ezreal and bury them in card advantage that way. At this point I have not lost the matchup, and I am honestly not even sure what their clean line to victory is if I play patiently.

#### Sideboarding

**Good cards**  
Retreat is excellent here, and Arcane Shift is a strong tool for catching up from behind if the game starts poorly. Singularity is also very strong, even when it is only killing one real card and a 1/1, because the matchup is mostly about keeping their board empty.

**Bad cards**  
Vex is the big loser again. I do not want large units here that line up badly against Singularity.

### Jax

**My evaluation:** Solidly favored, though they definitely have the tools to fight back.

#### Cards the Matchup Hinges On

**Rebuke vs. Not So Fast**  
Jax can invest enough resources into one unit that it stops lining up against Gust, and at that point I need a way to get it off the board cleanly. Rebuke is the best answer. If they try to build the same kind of giant unit two turns in a row, they usually have to spend Power re-equipping, and that opens the door for Fizz rebuying Rebuke to punish them.

**Lucian / First Mate**  
These are the cards that let them protect an attacker from the follow-up interaction I normally use to keep them off points. They can bring a unit back to base and blank a Windsinger line, so I want to kill Lucian on sight if it is vulnerable and stay aware of First Mate at all times.

**Jax**  
Jax himself is one of the few ways they can actually brute-force through my interaction. Deflect plus Quick-Draw equipment lines up well against a deck built around targeting units repeatedly, and the equipment leaves behind permanent advantage even when I answer the creature. The good news is that the Jax deck usually struggles to deploy Jax without falling far behind on points first.

#### General Gameplan

This matchup is about aggression. They have very few ready units, and their deck needs both equipment and creatures in play to function. I want to bounce their units as often as possible, because I do not want to get dragged into a long game where I am trying to run them out of cards while eventually dealing with Jax.

Singularity changes the shape of the matchup a lot. Once I can threaten it, they cannot really afford to tap out after the first few turns, which lets me create a large Energy advantage by forcing them to respect it while I keep spending my own Energy on their turn.

The games often look a lot like Irelia in miniature. I will lose some number of early fights over their units, but the moment they take a fight and lose, I usually pull so far ahead on points and tempo that they cannot recover.

#### Sideboarding

**Good cards**  
Smoke Screen is excellent against their larger equipment turns, and Rebuke is one of the best cards in the matchup because it lets me bounce oversized equipped units cleanly. Singularity is often weak if they play around it, but I still want access to it because if they ever tap below two Energy it can lock them out of the game completely. I just have to remember that Counterstrike lines up well against it, so even after resolving Rebuke I cannot assume Singularity is always safe. Vex is also solid here. They are a green counterspell deck that is weak to Rebuke, and Vex helps force through the kinds of targeting sequences I want. I just have to respect Challenge and try to use Vex for tempo rather than assuming it will live forever. If possible, I want Rebuke available before I expose Vex to Charm.

**Bad cards**  
There is not much I actively dislike in the matchup. I do trim a Fizz sometimes because the first Rebuke often does enough work on its own, and on the draw I am happy trimming a Plundering Poro because they often play equipment on turn 2, which makes a turn-1 Sneaky Deckhand opener more valuable.

### Rek'Sai

**My evaluation:** Surprisingly even, though I have not tested it against a truly expert pilot. Structurally they line up well against a tempo plan, but they are more vulnerable than they look to the grind plan built around Sneaky Deckhand closing the game.

#### Cards the Matchup Hinges On

**Falling Star / Piercing Light**  
These are the cards that stop me from simply overextending and trying to run them over quickly. I have to respect them the whole game.

**Rek'Sai's legend**  
If I am not careful, they will draw just as many extra cards as I do and I will eventually fall behind. I need to spend my spells in a way that forces their conquer to happen only after they have already spent their Energy for the turn. Gust is the best card for doing that, and even if they have Sun Disc I can still keep them from conquering on most turns.

**Immortal Phoenix**  
This is their inevitability, which means I do still need to score points. I cannot just sit there forever and assume that winning on cards will be enough.

#### General Gameplan

This matchup is mostly about defense, and I usually only want one unit in play at a time. The first job is to stop their legend from snowballing cards while still progressing my own points enough that they eventually have to walk into Bellows Breath blowouts.

Once I set up a solid Rune base, I can start deploying multiple units to base to tax Falling Star and score two points while trading with a Piercing Light. If I can run them out of units, Sneaky Deckhand plus hidden Windsinger becomes the cleanest way to pressure their removal while actually closing the game.

If they find multiple Immortal Phoenixes, I sometimes want to take a few turns with no units in play at all. In those games, the plan is to build toward one turn where I take both battlefields with Sneaky plus Windsinger while holding Bellows Breath and Frigid Touch to clear the Phoenixes afterward.

#### Sideboarding

**Good cards**  
Retreat is strong because it helps keep Immortal Phoenix in check by saving my units from bad exchanges. Singularity is also key for keeping their board under control, and Arcane Shift is a strong option for extra removal that can combo with Fizz to fully clear a board in the late game. Rocket Barrage would also be solid if I specifically wanted a Sun Disc answer, but in practice I would rather keep killing units and make them run out of cards worth using Sun Disc on.

**Bad cards**  
Ravenbloom Student and Vex are both weak here. I also think Pickpocket is a bait card in this matchup. I am fighting them on cards already, and I do not want a 3-cost unit that does not affect the board.

### Azir

**My evaluation:** Unfairly favored.

#### Cards the Matchup Hinges On

**Arise!**  
This is the card the matchup revolves around. My deck invalidates their core gameplan, so the main thing I need to do is apply enough pressure that they are forced to play defense with their Arise! army. Once that happens, Watcher becomes a clean way to punish them.

**Eye of the Herald**  
This is actually one of their best cards against me, because unlike most of their token setup, I cannot simply Gust the soldier away. I want to make sure I have a 2-Might unit at each battlefield whenever possible so I can eat those tokens for free.

#### General Gameplan

Gust and Windsinger function like hard removal in this matchup because almost all of their creatures are tokens. That means I get to play like the most broken control deck in the format, and the matchup becomes very straightforward.

The biggest rule is simple: do not let them have Azir in play when they cast Arise! If I follow that rule, I usually win easily.

Even B.F. Sword is not a huge problem. It takes their soldiers to 5 Might, which still lines up cleanly against Frigid Touch plus another spell.

#### Sideboarding

**Good cards**  
Singularity and Watcher are both excellent. Factory Recall or Arcane Shift are also strong if I have access to them, because they help me keep the board completely under control. Rebuke is another card I like a lot here. In this matchup it functions as a true kill spell, so I am happy to have the third copy and pay the Power for it.

**Bad cards**  
Vex is weak here. Beyond that, there is not much I actively dislike. Since they do not usually kill my units, I have often been trimming a Plundering Poro.

### Ornn

**My evaluation:** Relatively even with my current build. If I were playing Pickpockets and more gear removal, I would call the matchup favored.

#### Cards the Matchup Hinges On

**Ornn / Rebuke / Not So Fast**  
This is the core exchange of the matchup. Because of how much Deflect they get from Svellsongur, I cannot rely on the normal plan of just Rebuking the same threat over and over across a game. When Ornn gets going, I need Rebuke to matter at the right time, and I have to respect Not So Fast whenever I am trying to force the issue.

**Shurelya's Requiem**  
They do not always find the Ganking effects they need, but if they find Shurelya's Requiem the game gets much harder. That is the card that most often turns a manageable board into one I actually have to work to break through.

**Pit Crew**  
Not every list plays it, but if they do, I need to actually kill it. I cannot just let it sit around, because if it keeps generating points pressure then their score stops being manageable.

#### General Gameplan

Getting ahead early and hitting 6 before them is not hard, because they do not defend well in the early turns. If they do not find Shurelya's Requiem, the matchup is straightforward. I can usually just walk to the battlefield their Ornn is not at and hold my way to 8.

If they do find Shurelya's Requiem, the game changes. At that point I often need Rebuke on Ornn in order to score both battlefields and actually close the game.

#### Sideboarding

**Good cards**  
Rebuke, Vex, and Factory Recall are the first cards I want. Pickpocket is an all-star here because it often kills three Gears with a single card, which keeps Ornn small enough that I can simply Watcher over it. Smoke Screen is also better than Frigid Touch in this matchup because I am often using my shrink effects on the last turn of the game and want the stronger effect.

**Bad cards**  
Bellows Breath is the easy cut. I can keep their units under control well enough with Wages of Pain and Gust, and I would rather spend the early turns building Runes than trying to spawn-camp their relatively small number of cheap creatures.

### Fiora

**My evaluation:** Very favored.

#### Cards the Matchup Hinges On

**B.F. Sword**  
If they get B.F. Sword into play without falling behind, the matchup gets much harder. Once their units are big enough, bouncing them stops being efficient and I can lose control of the pace. I want to keep my foot on the gas so they never get a clean turn to spend on equipment.

**First Mate / Spectral Matron**  
This creates one of the awkward spots in the matchup. First Mate is one of the few cards I do not actually want to bounce, because its enter effect is such a strong tempo play. But if I kill First Mate too freely, Spectral Matron can bring it back and create a huge swing. If I can, I would much rather bounce First Mate on my own turn with Windsinger.

#### General Gameplan

This matchup is about getting ahead and staying ahead. They do not have many reaction-speed tricks, and they usually need to spend an early turn on equipment for their deck to function at full power. That gives me the window to get in front.

Bellows Breath and Singularity are the most important cards on my side for putting them irreparably behind. Singularity is especially strong because it keeps equipped creatures under control and punishes them for trying to build a board the normal way.

More broadly, they are missing the tools needed to really fight the reaction-speed battle this deck creates. Bellows Breath plus Watcher is the perfect pair of cards for punishing their build-an-army playstyle.

Remember that you can Stupefy their unit in response to an Equip to deny their legend trigger.

#### Sideboarding

**Good cards**  
Rebuke is excellent against their large equipped creatures. Watcher and Singularity are both great against their wider battlefield states, and Smoke Screen does a good job of counteracting oversized units. Factory Recall is also an all-star if I want it. Recalling a B.F. Sword sets up a perfect turn where I can Gust the unit and use my legend to keep pulling ahead.

**Bad cards**  
Vex is the easy cut here. My normal gameplan is already very good against them, so I do not need to expose myself to the downsides of Vex. If I need more room, I will often trim a Ravenbloom Student as well.

### Annie

**My evaluation:** Favored, but only if you know the matchup well.

#### Cards the Matchup Hinges On

**Fight or Flight / Flash / Long Sword**  
These are the cards that make the matchup dangerous. Annie's legend gives them the extra Energy to turn otherwise normal exchanges into tempo-positive ones, and these cards let them punish my Gusts and Wages of Pain if I am careless. Gust is one of the most important cards in the matchup because it lets me answer their reaction with one of my own.

**Singularity / Bellows Breath**  
These are my best cards for actually getting their units off the board through their movement effects. If I am going to stabilize, it is usually because one of these cards creates the swing turn.

#### General Gameplan

This is the matchup where I have to forget most of the normal identity of the deck and just play control. Their legend is so tempo positive that I cannot afford to spend resources being aggressive early. My job is to keep their points low, use Gust to manage Forerunner tokens and awkward combat steps, and eventually run them out of cardboard.

Once I get the game to that stage, the matchup becomes much easier. They are built to keep pressing, not to recover after I have answered wave after wave of their units.

#### Sideboarding

**Good cards**  
I want as many direct interaction cards as possible. Smoke Screen is useful because it helps me hold against Kai'Sa or Darius-sized threats, and Vex gives me a cleaner way to hold battlefields once I am stable. More than anything, I want my hand to be mostly spells, because if I keep interacting they will eventually run out of units.

**Bad cards**  
Sneaky Deckhand and Plundering Poro are both much worse here because this is not a matchup where I want to be spending resources trying to get ahead early. Rebuke is also weaker than usual, though I still like keeping one or two copies because it helps fight Forerunners and keeps my tempo high against Darius turns.

### Aurora

**My evaluation:** Favored, but very losable.

#### Cards the Matchup Hinges On

**Aurora**  
These decks simply cannot put enough units into play to contest me without Aurora. I am going to hit 6 before them almost every game, and the real question is whether they get enough traction off an Aurora turn to stop me from closing.

**Deadbloom Predator**  
Without this card, the matchup would be close to free. Coming straight to a battlefield usually means I have to Rebuke it immediately, and the hardest games are the ones where they hit a Deadbloom Predator off Aurora, force the Rebuke, then get to replay Predator on the following turn while also using Aurora to put another creature directly onto a battlefield.

#### General Gameplan

This matchup is about aggression. If they are not ramping into a fast Aurora, they lose. That means I want to take points as quickly as possible and kill their units the moment they show up at base, so they do not get clean Aurora turns that place threats directly onto battlefields.

There are two common versions of the deck, and they play a little differently.

Against **Miss Fortune Aurora**, the scary line is Aurora into **Mindsplitter**. Because I do not play Called Shot, that often means my best answer is not available and I need my second-best answer instead. That is one of the biggest reasons I lean toward **Factory Recall** over **Rocket Barrage** when I want gear hate.

Against **Volibear Aurora**, the matchup shifts because they play multiple removal spells that only function if they already have a unit in play. That means I want to use Bellows Breath and Singularity to spawn-camp them and never let them take an Action before their unit is dead.

#### Sideboarding

**Good cards**  
Factory Recall is one of the best cards here. Smoke Screen and Rebuke are also important because they help me fight their larger units cleanly. Vex is good against Miss Fortune Aurora, though I do not like it against Volibear Aurora.

**Bad cards**  
Wages of Pain is weaker here. Bellows Breath is also weaker against Miss Fortune Aurora specifically.

## Card Options and Flex Slots

### Maindeck Card Options

Most of the maindeck is relatively fixed, but there are a few real flex slots and a little give within each package. The core of the deck looks like this:

- 11 cheap units: 3 Windsinger, 3 Plundering Poro, 3 Sneaky Deckhand, 2 flexible 2-drops
- 6 shrink effects: 3 Stupefy, 2 Frigid Touch, 1 flexible shrink slot
- 9 interaction spells: 3 Gust, 3 Wages of Pain, 3 Bellows Breath
- 5 consistency cards: 3 Stacked Deck, 2 Fizz
- 6 top-end cards: 1 Thousand-Tailed Watcher, 1 Singularity, 2 Rebuke, 2 flexible late-game slots

That leaves two true flex slots, plus some room to tune each package depending on the metagame.

#### Unit Flexibility

The main unit flex is usually **Ravenbloom Student** versus **Treasure Hunter**.

Ravenbloom Student is an objectively strong card, but it does not naturally play to what this deck is trying to do. Its power shows up later in the game, and this deck is not trying to hold with raw Might at any point. I cannot rely on a single shrink effect to hold, but Student does have an important use case. Especially with Stupefy, a Student on defense can force the opponent to blink first and let me react to their pump spell with Gust.

Treasure Hunter pulls in the opposite direction. It is much better on turn 2 and helps set up the late game by generating Gold, which this deck is very good at using. It also guarantees Gold against Action Speed removal in a way that Plundering Poro does not, which makes it the better 2-drop in control matchups.

I leaned toward Student in my list because I expected Rek'Sai to be popular as an anti-Irelia choice in last week's metagame. In general, this deck does not want truly dead late-game cards like Plundering Poro or Treasure Hunter, but I would happily play up to two Treasure Hunter depending on the metagame.

#### Shrink Flexibility

The shrink package has a little room, but not much.

Drawing multiple Frigid Touches can be extremely awkward. The card is expensive, and when I have to repeat it just to turn on one of my interaction spells, it becomes one of the lowest-tempo plays in the deck.

Because of that, in a heavy equipment metagame I would swap the third Frigid Touch into **Smoke Screen**.

#### Late-Game Slots

This is the part of the maindeck I change most often. The main choices are **Thousand-Tailed Watcher**, **Singularity**, **Vex**, and **Arcane Shift**, and which mix I want depends almost entirely on what I expect to play against.

**Thousand-Tailed Watcher** is the most generically strong option. I am happy to draw one in most games, and in an open metagame I like having access to two because it is my cleanest answer to untargetable-by-spells threats.

**Singularity** is truly backbreaking against midrange decks, though it loses some value against players who use Ferrous Forerunner well and do not expose too much to it.

**Vex** is an extremely targeted anti-Deflect card. I avoid maindecking it whenever I think I can get away with that, but sadly it is necessary if I expect a lot of Irelia.

**Arcane Shift** is my best card against anything small-ball, and with Fizz it gives me a way to grind through Ferrous Forerunner in the late game once I have ten or more Runes.

Going forward, my default recommendation for the late-game package is **2 Watcher, 1 Singularity, 1 Arcane Shift**.

#### True Flex Slots

I lean toward the third **Fizz** almost all the time because it does so much to improve the consistency of the two-card combinations the deck is built around. A lot of matchups come down to casting a second Rebuke or a second Bellows Breath to really close the door, and with all of the Gust synergy I usually want a Fizz in every game.

The other real flex point is the **ninth 2-drop**. Adding a ninth 2-drop takes the deck from roughly **82% to 86%** to have a turn-1 play on the play, which is a meaningful consistency bump but nowhere near mandatory. My plan going forward is to turn that slot into **Sprite Call** instead. Sprite Call adds directly to the deck's ability to score points, which helps offset the slightly lower odds of always having a turn-1 unit.

**Sprite Call** is my pet card. It is bad on Reaver's Row and bad into Obelisk of Power decks that do not play many 2-drops, which is why it was awkward in the previous metagame. But like Sneaky Deckhand, it can score a point without costing Runes, and instead of leaving a body behind it kills the opponent's 2-drop. Post-ban, I expect to play two Sprite Calls as a high-impact defensive early-game card that also incidentally scores and can be bought back with Fizz for more points later.

#### Notable Excludes

The two big excludes are **Called Shot** and **Purple Seal**.

This deck wants a wide range of interactive options in the early game, so it cannot afford a card draw spell that costs Runes. It also does not want to spend hand space on Purple Seal just to enable a stronger late game. For that reason, this is not a Called Shot deck.

I tested Called Shot a lot, and it always led to the same outcomes. Either I was kicking it on the last turn of the game just to dig, or I was casting it out of desperation to find the spells I needed to keep up early, recycling Runes in the process, and then losing anyway.

### Battlefield Options

If you just want my post-ban recommendation, keep it simple: **Arena's Greatest** on the play, **Fortified Position** on the draw, and **Marai Spire** or **Emperor's Dais** for game 1.

Reaver's Row used to be the best draw battlefield for this deck because it protected our units from combat, and I am very glad it is gone. The good news is that this deck still has excellent options, and the right battlefield package is one of the easiest ways to tune the list for a metagame. Some are flexible play or draw which makes them good game 1 battlefields, while others shine in one situation or the other.

#### Arena's Greatest (Play)

This is my default play battlefield. When this deck starts first, it is unreasonably good at closing games quickly, and no deck really keeps up with our tempo on the play. Arena's Greatest helps make sure the opponent never gets enough time to replay all of the units I bounce and drag the game out.

#### Fortified Position (Draw)

This is my go-to draw battlefield now that Reaver's Row is gone. The only part of my gameplan that really leans on combat is Thousand-Tailed Watcher, and once Watcher shrinks a unit it is easy to Gust the unit targeted by the Shield trigger before it ever gains Shield.

Fortified Position does a great job of protecting a turn-1 Sneaky Deckhand against most decks, and because the deck is so spell-oriented I can often subvert the battlefield trigger instead of being constrained by it.

#### Void Gate (Game 1)

Honestly, this battlefield is bad and very low impact, but it is still an excellent game 1 choice if the goal is to look like control Ezreal. It used to be especially good against Miracle Draven because it helped Bellows Breath clear their holds, but with both of those decks gone I am largely off it.

#### Treasure Hoard (Game 1)

My deck can be turbo-charged by even one or two extra Gold, and it is naturally set up to punish opponents who spend Energy early trying to generate Gold themselves. That makes Treasure Hoard a very real option for game 1.

#### Hall of Legends (Game 1)

Hall of Legends is playable in any game. Against most opponents it is relatively asymmetrical, but I trigger my legend more often on the opponent's turn than my own, which lowers the ceiling a little. It is solid, but I think the deck has stronger options.

#### Marai Spire (Game 1)

Marai Spire is very strong. Reducing Frigid Touch in the early game matters a lot, and it also helps set up turns involving Frigid Touch, Bellows Breath, and Ezreal. It is a little harder to use on the draw, but it is playable in any game and tends to be highly asymmetrical in real matchups.

#### Emperor's Dais (Game 1)

This battlefield is extremely fun and very game-warping. Walking Windsinger here and returning it to hand so I have the perfect answer when the opponent tries to make tokens is extremely strong. Against some control decks, they simply cannot beat repeated Sneaky Deckhand turns here, because I can play Sneaky and rebuy it every late-game turn.

I think this is a strong game 1 battlefield. On the draw, repeated Windsinger triggers are an excellent way to catch up, and on the play it is disgusting to play Sneaky Deckhand and immediately rebuy it on turn 2.

#### Bait Battlefields

**Targon's Peak**  
I played this for a while because it looks unfair, and on the play it absolutely can create broken-looking openings. The problem is that my core gameplay loop against most decks involves losing a fight on their turn, then bouncing the unit on my own turn. I do not want to give the opponent extra Energy to fight back on that axis. It is especially bad against Irelia, and that matchup more than anything convinced me that Targon's Peak is not what this deck wants.

**Ravenbloom Conservatory**  
This is another battlefield that looks better than it plays. The deck is spells-heavy, but in practice I do not defend very often and I actually play a lot of units, so I do not get paid off for it the way I want to.

### High Impact Sideboard Cards

I usually want to spend two or three sideboard slots on cards that are completely maindeckable, high-impact late-game effects. This deck really wants to have the right top end for each matchup, but it also cannot afford to play every card it wants in the maindeck. The third Rebuke, second Watcher, second Singularity, or extra copies of cards like Vex and Arcane Shift all let me tune the top end correctly for a given field.

Once those slots are accounted for, there are still a lot of strong options depending on how the metagame develops.

#### Gear Hate

**Factory Recall** and **Rocket Barrage** are the main gear-hate options I look at. Recall is usually stronger, because in this deck all I really want is spells that shrink the opposing creature by removing its equipment. Against Aurora specifically, Factory Recall often buys enough time that I can set up a winning turn right when they try to redeploy Aurora.

#### Pickpocket

I do not think this card is good in this deck. Against Seal decks, my goal is usually to run them out of cards, not to fight over the Seals themselves. A 3-Energy unit that does not affect the board is not what I am looking for.

The one real exception is Ornn, where Pickpocket can keep Ornn small enough that I can just Watcher over it. Even then, that is too narrow for me right now.

#### Retreat

Retreat is a strong anti-control option. It plays well in the early game and stays flexible later, whether I want to be aggressive with Sneaky Deckhand or generate value by rebuying Fizz or Ezreal. My deck also uses the extra Rune very well. I am still not fully convinced I need the help, though, and it is a little low-impact specifically against Kai'Sa.

#### Dr. Mundo

This is a real control hoser, but without Called Shot it usually only generates two or three hold points before it is in range of removal. I generally lean away from it, but if Kai'Sa becomes more popular I could absolutely see playing one.

#### Morbid Return

This is another anti-control option, and it is one of the most miserable things I can do if I choose to lean into it. If I ever want to play for the absolute longest late game, Morbid Return plus Fizz lets me set up loops involving Deckhand or other key effects turn after turn. It is miserable, but it is also extremely hard for control decks to beat.

#### Hard Bargain

Hard Bargain is very narrow, but the places where it is good, it is excellent. It helps a lot in matchups where I am trying to squeeze the opponent's resources. It can let me score multiple points while countering a Wages of Pain in the midgame, and it is one of my best tools for helping Rebuke resolve against Irelia.

That said, more than any other sideboard card, this is the one that has sat in my hand and directly contributed to losses because I did not have enough targeting effects to keep my legend drawing cards. I would never maindeck it, and I do not bring it in casually. I only want it for Rebuke counter wars and blue removal-heavy matchups.

#### Smoke Screen

Whenever the matchup is about the opponent making medium-sized units, Smoke Screen is excellent. Frigid Touch costs a lot of Energy, so it matters a lot to have access to one or two copies of "no, really, just be smaller" even if it costs Power.

#### Unchecked Power

Unchecked Power is the big battlefield sweeper. It is excellent against hold decks and against untargetable-by-spells threats, but I usually do not feel like I need it because I already have access to Watcher plus Bellows Breath. It also asks a lot of the rest of your sideboard, because the main decks where you want it are often playing **Guardian Angel** and **Zhonya's Hourglass**, so if I wanted to rely on Unchecked Power I would also want access to Factory Recall.
